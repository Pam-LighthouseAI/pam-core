## Executive Summary

**Recommendation: YES — Tenant Rights should be a separate category in My Civic Voice.**

Tenant rights is a high-demand issue affecting millions of Canadian renters. With Canada's housing crisis, rising rents, and eviction concerns, this is one of the most pressing civic issues. The jurisdiction is clear (primarily provincial), making it easy to route users correctly. The existing "Housing & Development" category covers property taxes, zoning, and development but doesn't adequately address the tenant-landlord relationship and dispute resolution.

## Common Problems Tenants Face in Canada

### 1. Rent Increases
- **Scope**: National concern, especially in provinces without rent control
- **Details**: Alberta, Saskatchewan, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, and Newfoundland have NO rent control — landlords can increase rent by any amount with proper notice
- **Provincial variation**: Ontario (2.5% guideline for 2025), BC (annual limit), Quebec (must be "reasonable" and justifiable)
- **Impact**: 45% of Canadians report being "very concerned" about housing affordability; ~20% of households are housing cost-burdened (spending >30% of income on housing)

### 2. Evictions
- **Types**: Non-payment, landlord's own use, renovations ("renovictions"), demolition, sale of property
- **Renovictions**: Bad-faith evictions where landlords claim renovations to remove tenants and raise rents — major issue in tight rental markets
- **Cash for keys**: Landlords paying tenants to vacate voluntarily — controversial practice
- **No-fault evictions**: BC has highest rate of no-fault evictions in Canada

### 3. Maintenance and Repairs
- **Issue**: Landlords failing to address repair requests, maintenance issues
- **Legal obligation**: All provinces require landlords to maintain habitable premises
- **Enforcement**: Tribunals can order repairs, rent abatement
- **Common problems**: Mold, pest infestations, heating/cooling failures, plumbing issues

### 4. Security Deposits
- **Provincial variation**: 
  - Quebec: NO security deposits allowed
  - BC: Maximum half-month's rent
  - Alberta: Up to one month's rent
  - Ontario: Only last month's rent deposit allowed (not a damage deposit)
- **Disputes**: Return of deposits, deductions for damages, interest payments

### 5. Privacy and Harassment
- **Landlord entry**: Most provinces require 24-48 hours' notice for non-emergency entry
- **Harassment**: Intimidation, excessive communication, threats
- **Quiet enjoyment**: Fundamental tenant right in all provinces

### 6. Tribunal Backlogs and Access to Justice
- **Ontario LTB**: 53,000+ unresolved cases as of 2024; 90% are landlords waiting for hearings
- **Impact**: Tenants in limbo waiting for dispute resolution; landlords going without rental income
- **National issue**: Most provinces experiencing tribunal delays

### 7. Discrimination in Housing
- **Protected grounds**: Race, disability, family status, age, religion, sexual orientation, etc.
- **Human rights**: Federal and provincial human rights codes protect tenants from discrimination
- **Issues**: Refusal to rent, differential treatment, inaccessible housing

### 8. Illegal Lockouts and Evictions
- **Issue**: Landlords physically removing tenants or changing locks without legal process
- **Rights**: Only sheriffs/bailiffs can physically remove tenants after legal eviction order
- **Recourse**: Tribunals, human rights commissions, legal aid

## Jurisdiction Analysis

### Federal Role
- **Limited**: Federal government has minimal direct role in residential tenancies
- **Areas**: 
  - CMHC affordable housing programs
  - National Housing Strategy
  - Canada Housing Benefit (with provinces)
  - Tax credits (e.g., GST New Residential Rental Rebate)
  - Human rights (Canadian Human Rights Act for federal jurisdiction)

### Provincial Role (PRIMARY)
- **Main jurisdiction**: Residential tenancies are primarily provincial
- **Responsibilities**:
  - Residential tenancy legislation
  - Rent control (where applicable)
  - Security deposit rules
  - Eviction procedures
  - Landlord-tenant tribunals
  - Maintenance and repair standards
  - Human rights in housing (provincial codes)

### Municipal Role
- **Limited but important**:
  - Rental licensing (some cities)
  - Property standards bylaws
  - Zoning for rental housing
  - Renoviction bylaws (Toronto, some BC cities)
  - Tenant protection bylaws

## Recommendation for My Civic Voice

### Should Tenant Rights Be a Category?

**YES — Strong recommendation to add as a separate category.**

### Rationale

1. **High Demand**: Housing is a top-of-mind issue for Canadians. With the housing crisis, rent burden, and eviction concerns, this affects millions.

2. **Clear Jurisdiction**: Tenant rights are primarily PROVINCIAL, with some FEDERAL involvement in affordable housing programs and human rights, and MUNICIPAL involvement in property standards and bylaws. Easy to route correctly.

3. **Not Adequately Covered**: The existing "Housing & Development" category focuses on:
   - Affordable housing programs (federal/provincial/municipal)
   - Property taxes (municipal)
   - Zoning & development permits (municipal)
   - Homelessness services (municipal/provincial/federal)
   - Rent control (provincial)
   - Building permits (municipal)
   - Short-term rentals (municipal)
   
   It does NOT adequately cover:
   - Tenant-landlord disputes
   - Eviction proceedings
   - Security deposit issues
   - Maintenance and repair complaints
   - Tribunal access
   - Discrimination in housing

4. **Actionable**: Users can contact:
   - Provincial representatives about tenancy law reform
   - Provincial tribunals for dispute resolution
   - Human rights commissions for discrimination
   - Municipal councils about tenant protection bylaws

5. **Distinct User Journey**: A tenant facing eviction or a rent dispute has different needs than someone concerned about zoning or property taxes.

## Conclusion

Tenant rights is a critical issue affecting millions of Canadian renters. Adding a dedicated "Tenant Rights & Renting" category to My Civic Voice would:

1. **Address a major gap** in the current issue categorization
2. **Provide clear routing** to provincial tenancy tribunals and representatives
3. **Empower users** with information about their rights
4. **Differentiate from "Housing & Development"** which focuses on development, zoning, and programs rather than tenant-landlord relationships

The sub-issues proposed cover the most common tenant concerns and clearly indicate which level of government to contact. Implementation would require adding the category, sub-issues, French translations, and email template as outlined above.
